History

üìú The History of Dumplings

Dumplings are one of China's oldest traditional foods, with a history spanning over 1,800 years. Legend has it that during the late Eastern Han Dynasty, the renowned physician Zhang Zhongjing wrapped lamb and medicinal herbs in thin dough and boiled them to treat villagers suffering from frostbitten ears during winter. These early dumplings were called "Jiao Er" (Â®áËÄ≥), meaning "tender ears," for their distinctive ear-like shape.

By the Tang and Song dynasties, dumplings had evolved from medicinal food into an everyday staple, spreading widely among common people. During the Ming and Qing dynasties, they became an essential dish for major holidays ‚Äî especially the Lunar New Year ‚Äî a tradition that continues to this day.

In northern China, dumplings symbolize reunion, wealth, and good fortune. Their crescent shape resembles ancient gold ingots, representing prosperity. Some families even tuck a coin inside one dumpling ‚Äî whoever finds it is said to enjoy good luck in the coming year.

The tradition of eating dumplings on the Winter Solstice is especially strong in northern China. A popular saying goes: "If you don't eat dumplings on the Winter Solstice, your ears will freeze off!" ‚Äî a nod to Zhang Zhongjing's legendary remedy.

Step 2

Making the Dough

The quality of the wrapper starts with good dough ‚Äî it should be smooth, pliable, and well-rested.

  1. Pour 300g of all-purpose flour into a large bowl and make a well in the center.
  2. Slowly pour in about 150ml of warm water (~40¬∞C / 104¬∞F), stirring with chopsticks until shaggy clumps form.
  3. Knead by hand until the dough becomes smooth and elastic, about 10 minutes.
  4. Cover with plastic wrap and let the dough rest at room temperature for 30 minutes.
To test if the dough is ready: press a finger into it ‚Äî it should spring back slowly. This means it's the right consistency.
Step 3

Preparing the Filling

A flavorful, juicy filling is the heart of a great dumpling. The key technique is "adding water" to keep the meat tender.

  1. Finely chop the cabbage, toss with a pinch of salt, and let sit for 10 minutes. Squeeze out the excess moisture to prevent a watery filling.
  2. Mix ground pork with minced scallion and ginger, soy sauce, a splash of dark soy sauce, salt, and white pepper.
  3. Add scallion-ginger water (or plain water) in 3 small additions, stirring in the same direction each time until the mixture becomes sticky and holds together.
  4. Fold in the drained cabbage and sesame oil, and mix gently until just combined.
Always stir in one direction ‚Äî this is key! The filling is ready when it becomes sticky and clings to the chopsticks.
Step 4

Rolling the Wrappers

The ideal wrapper is slightly thicker in the center and thinner at the edges ‚Äî this prevents tearing when folding.

  1. Roll the rested dough into a long log and cut into small pieces, about 10g each (roughly thumb-sized).
  2. Flatten each piece, then roll from the center outward with a rolling pin, rotating the dough slightly after each roll.
  3. Aim for a round wrapper about 8‚Äì9cm (3‚Äì3.5 inches) in diameter, thinner at the edges and thicker in the middle.
Dust the work surface and dough pieces lightly with flour to prevent sticking. Roll in a rotating motion ‚Äî not back and forth ‚Äî to keep the shape round.
Step 5

Wrapping Dumplings

There are many ways to fold a dumpling. Here are three popular styles:

ü§è Classic Crescent Place filling in the center, fold in half, and pleat from the middle outward ‚Äî 5 to 7 folds per side.
üëå Gold Ingot Make the crescent shape first, then bring the two ends together and pinch to form a ring ‚Äî like a traditional gold ingot.
‚úåÔ∏è Simple Fold Place filling in the center, fold in half, and press the edges firmly to seal. Quick and easy ‚Äî perfect for beginners.
Don't overfill ‚Äî about one teaspoon of filling is enough. Leave about 1cm of edge around the filling for a tight seal. Press firmly so they don't burst while cooking.
Step 6

Cooking the Dumplings

The classic boiling method uses the "three boils" technique ‚Äî adding cold water three times ‚Äî so the wrappers cook through without bursting.

  1. Bring a large pot of water to a boil over high heat. Add a pinch of salt to help prevent sticking.
  2. Gently slide the dumplings in and stir slowly with a wooden spoon to keep them from sticking to the bottom.
  3. When the water returns to a boil, add half a cup of cold water. Repeat this step 3 times total.
  4. After the third addition, once the water boils again and the dumplings are floating and puffed up, they're ready to serve.
Keep the heat on medium-high throughout. The "three cold water additions" prevent the skins from breaking. A drizzle of sesame oil after draining keeps them from sticking together.
Step 7

Dipping Sauce

A great dipping sauce brings everything together. Here are three classic combinations:

  1. Classic: 2 tbsp soy sauce + 1 tbsp rice vinegar + a drizzle of chili oil. Simple and satisfying.
  2. Garlic: Add 2 cloves of minced garlic to the classic sauce for a bold, aromatic kick.
  3. Sesame: Soy sauce + vinegar + 1 tsp sesame paste + a pinch of sugar. Rich and nutty.
Adjust the vinegar ratio to your preference. Some people love a tangier sauce; others prefer it more savory. There's no wrong answer!
